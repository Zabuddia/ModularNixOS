Beginnings

Minimalism is an art movement that emerged in New York in the late 1950s and early 1960s. Minimalist artists wanted to renounce the recent art that they felt had become stale and academic. This led them to remove metaphors and expression from their art and create objects that didn't look like fine art.

Definition

Minimalist art is characterized by the idea that art should not be an imitation of something else, but should be its own reality. Usually we think of art as a representation of something else, whether that is something physically in the world or a feeling or emotion. With minimalism, however, there is no attempt to represent something else. The artist only wants the viewer to see and think of what is in front of them. Frank Stella, a famous minimalist painter, once described this as, "What you see is what you see."

End

While the minimalist art movement has not entirely disappeared, it's popularity has definitely decreased. This decrease in popularity began in the late 1970s when people started to criticize minimalist art for its lack of emotional depth and its detachment from reality. This led to people shifting towards "postminimalism", which was a movement where people made more dynamic and expressive forms of art. Although most artists have gone away from the traditional minimalist style, the minimalist movement set the stage for more artistic expression in modern culture. The legacy of minimalism continues today.

Key Ideas

  • Reduction - Stripping away details to focus on basic forms.
  • Simplicity - Using clean lines, repeated shapes, and limited color.
  • Material - Emphasizing the real materials instead of illusion.
  • Space - Letting empty space become part of the work.
  • Viewer - Inviting the viewer to notice how the work feels in space.
